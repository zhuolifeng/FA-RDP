Abstract

Contact-rich manipulation needs two conflicting things within one episode. Before contact, many trajectories are equally valid, so the policy should keep diverse action modes. After contact, force and geometry narrow the options, and success depends on reacting quickly to force feedback. Standard diffusion policies run at one fixed inference frequency, forcing a compromise: low-frequency sampling preserves pre-contact modes but reacts slowly, while high-frequency sampling reacts fast but collapses those modes. We present FA-RDP, a frequency-adaptive reactive diffusion policy. A shared multi-frequency visual-force Transformer predicts action chunks at both low and high frequencies, and a learned multimodality indicator switches between multi-step low-frequency sampling before contact and one-step high-frequency sampling after contact. We further introduce Manifold Consistency Distillation (MCD) for stable one-step prediction. On three contact-rich tasks, FA-RDP reaches the highest success rate while keeping diverse pre-contact modes. Code will be made publicly available.

Method

FA-RDP has three parts that together resolve the multimodality–reactivity tradeoff.

1 · Multi-Frequency Visual-Force Transformer

Dense positions for high-frequency chunks, sparse positions for low-frequency chunks, on one shared temporal grid.

One shared backbone serves both frequencies. Frequency-adaptive positional encoding picks dense temporal positions for high-frequency chunks and sparse positions for low-frequency chunks — no extra tokens. A causal action-force mask blocks future-contact leakage.

2 · Multimodality-Based Frequency Selection

A lightweight indicator head reads the visual tokens and outputs a scalar confidence. When confidence is low (pre-contact, many modes), FA-RDP samples multi-step at low frequency; when it is high (post-contact), it switches to one-step high-frequency sampling.

3 · Manifold Consistency Distillation (MCD)

MCD predicts actions on the robot action manifold for stable one-step inference.

Distilling noise-like targets is unstable for robot actions. MCD instead has the student predict action chunks directly on the robot action manifold, keeping DDPM residual supervision while producing smooth, valid outputs — which makes reliable one-step inference possible.

Experiments

Experimental Setup

Flexiv Rizon 4s arm with wrist and third-view cameras. Three contact-rich tasks: box flipping, switch toggling, button pressing.

Q1 · Comparison with Baselines

We compare FA-RDP against four baselines on all three tasks (20 trials each). FA-RDP wins on every task and reaches 81.7% average success, about 30 points above the strongest baseline, ImplicitRDP (51.7%). Plain diffusion (DP) reacts too slowly to force and fails almost entirely on Box Flipping; the regression policy is fast but loses pre-contact diversity, so it drifts to the wrong contact point. FA-RDP is the only method that both approaches correctly and reacts fast enough to stay in contact through the task.

Q2 · Pre-Contact Multimodality Preservation

Does frequency adaptation actually keep the pre-contact modes? On Box Flipping there are four valid ways to approach the box. Over repeated rollouts, FA-RDP uses all four, matching the full multi-step policy, while running one-step high-frequency sampling everywhere collapses to a single dominant mode. This confirms that switching to low-frequency sampling before contact is what preserves diverse behavior — speed alone would throw it away.

Q3 · Indicator Accuracy & Force Curves

Does the indicator switch frequency at the right moment? We plot the indicator value against the measured contact force over an episode. The indicator stays low while the arm is still approaching and rises sharply the moment contact force appears — so the policy runs low-frequency sampling exactly during the free-approach phase and one-step high-frequency sampling exactly during contact. Using this learned switch instead of always running high frequency raises average success from 61.7% to 81.7%.

The indicator stays low before contact and rises after contact, triggering the switch from low- to high-frequency sampling. Switching lifts average success from 61.7% to 81.7%.

Q4 · Distillation Method Comparison

Does MCD give better one-step high-frequency prediction than other distillation objectives? All methods run in high-frequency mode with frequency selection off, so this isolates the distillation choice. The high-frequency-alone policy is the multi-step policy simply run one-step with no distillation; the others apply different one-step distillation objectives. Standard distillation targets fail badly here: MeanFlow and Consistency almost never succeed (1.7%) because they must predict noise-like quantities that are unstable for robot actions, and naive one-step sampling reaches only 20.0%. MCD predicts on the action manifold instead and reaches 61.7% — more than 3× the next best — showing that where the target lives matters more than the distillation loss itself.
