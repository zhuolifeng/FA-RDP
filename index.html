Abstract

In contact-rich manipulation, action multimodality and reactivity dominate different stages of a single episode. Before contact, multiple trajectories might be equally valid, making it important to preserve diverse action modes. After contact, geometric constraints and force limits narrow the solution space, while successful execution demands rapid responses to force feedback. However, standard diffusion policies use a fixed inference frequency and sampling steps throughout the episode, forcing a fundamental compromise: low-frequency, multi-step sampling better preserves pre-contact multimodality but responds slowly to force feedback, whereas high-frequency sampling improves reactivity but tends to collapse distinct pre-contact modes. To resolve this tradeoff, we present FA-RDP, a frequency-adaptive reactive diffusion policy. A shared multi-frequency visual-force Transformer predicts action chunks at both low and high frequencies, while a learned multimodality indicator dynamically selects multi-step low-frequency sampling before contact and one-step high-frequency sampling as action ambiguity decreases. We further introduce Manifold Consistency Distillation (MCD), which reparameterizes the diffusion network to predict actions on the robot action manifold while retaining DDPM-based residual supervision. Experiments on three contact-rich manipulation tasks show that FA-RDP achieves the highest success rate while preserving diverse pre-contact trajectory modes. Code will be made publicly available.

Method

FA-RDP consists of three tightly integrated components that together resolve the multimodality–reactivity tradeoff inherent to contact-rich manipulation.

1 · Multi-Frequency Visual-Force Transformer

Multi-frequency visual-force Transformer. Dense positions for high-frequency chunks; sparse positions for low-frequency chunks — on a shared temporal grid.

A single shared backbone handles both frequency modes through frequency-adaptive positional encoding: the frequency mode selects temporal indices on a shared grid rather than appending an extra token — high-frequency mode uses dense consecutive positions, low-frequency mode uses sparse positions.

The backbone is trained jointly with a multi-frequency diffusion objective $\mathcal{L}_\text{MF} = \mathcal{L}_\epsilon^\ell + \mathcal{L}_\epsilon^h$, where the network predicts action chunks on the robot manifold and epsilon supervision is recovered via a fixed reparameterization.

A causal action-force mask prevents future contact leakage while preserving efficient parallel training.

2 · Multimodality-Based Frequency Selection

A lightweight Transformer multimodality indicator head maps slow visual tokens to a scalar confidence $\sigma(\mathbf{o}_n)$. During Stage 2, we freeze the backbone and calibrate the indicator by sampling the low-frequency policy $N=8$ times per observation and computing an empirical action residual:

$e(\mathbf{o}_n) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=1}^{N}\|\hat{\mathbf{A}}^\ell_m - \mathbf{A}^\ell\|_2^2$

At inference, if $\sigma > \tau$ (contact phase, low ambiguity) FA-RDP selects the one-step high-frequency distilled sampler; otherwise it uses multi-step low-frequency DDIM to preserve diverse pre-contact modes.

3 · Manifold Consistency Distillation (MCD)

MCD maps noisy states to action manifold predictions. Stop-gradient target alignment + sample regression loss (SRL) stabilize one-step inference.

Directly distilling epsilon, score, or velocity targets is unstable for robot action prediction. MCD instead reparameterizes the student to predict action chunks on the robot action manifold, converting noise-like targets into smooth, kinematically valid outputs.

The Stage 3 distillation objective combines a consistency alignment term with a sample regression loss (SRL):

$\mathcal{L}_\text{distill} = \lambda_\text{MCD}\|\hat{\mathbf{A}}_\theta^h - \text{sg}[\hat{\mathbf{A}}_{\bar\theta}^h]\|^2 + \lambda_\text{SRL}\|\hat{\mathbf{A}}_\theta^h - \mathbf{A}^h\|^2$

The fixed DDPM conversion retains residual supervision without forcing the network to predict noise-like quantities, significantly reducing learning difficulty.

Experiments

Experimental Setup

Flexiv Rizon 4s follower arm with wrist-mounted iPhone fisheye camera and third-view USB camera. Three contact-rich tasks designed to test both multimodality and reactivity.

Main Results — Q1: Comparison with Baselines

Method Box Flipping Switch Toggling Button Pressing Avg.
DP 0/204/202/2010.0%
RDP 5/209/207/2035.0%
ImplicitRDP 8/2012/2011/2051.7%
Regression Policy w/ Force 2/206/204/2020.0%
FA-RDP (Ours) 14/2017/2018/2081.7%

FA-RDP achieves 81.7% average success rate — 30 percentage points above the strongest baseline (ImplicitRDP).

Failure Case Analysis

Dual Box Flipping failure cases. DP and ImplicitRDP lose contact during the flip; RDP contacts the wrong box region; Regression Policy w/ Force knocks down a front block. FA-RDP keeps contact and completes the task.

Dual Switch Toggling failure cases. DP and ImplicitRDP lose contact during the toggle; RDP contacts the wrong switch region; Regression Policy w/ Force knocks down a front block. FA-RDP keeps contact and completes the toggle.

Dual Button Pressing failure cases. DP and ImplicitRDP lose contact during the press; RDP contacts the wrong button region; Regression Policy w/ Force knocks down a front block. FA-RDP keeps contact and completes the press.
